# Steve Jobs — Chief Design & Brand Officer

## Product Naming
**"Patterns."**

That's it. Not "Board Review Insights" or "Lessons from AI Advisors." Just **Patterns**.

This post reveals what emerges when you run the same gauntlet 11 times. Jensen's obsession with moats. Warren's ruthless unit economics. The disagreements that matter. One word. Unmistakable.

## Design Philosophy: Clarity Through Repetition
This isn't another "AI is cool" thinkpiece. This is **empirical magic** — the kind of truth that only reveals itself through repetition.

The design principle: **Show, don't summarize.** Real quotes. Real disagreements. Real patterns. No hand-waving about "AI insights." The reader should FEEL the personalities colliding — Jensen hammering on competitive advantage while Oprah probes emotional truth.

The layout must breathe. Each persona gets a signature visual identity. When Warren speaks, you SEE the folksy wisdom. When Shonda critiques, you FEEL the narrative tension.

## User Experience: The First 30 Seconds
You land on the page. Immediately, you see four faces arranged like a board table.

Then: **one devastating pattern** right at the top. Something like:

> "In 11 board reviews, Jensen Huang asked about competitive moats **11 times**. Warren Buffett asked about unit economics **11 times**. Shonda Rhimes asked about retention hooks **10 times**. They agreed on a green light exactly **twice**."

THAT'S the hook. No preamble. No "In today's rapidly evolving AI landscape..." Just: here's what happened when we stress-tested ideas against the best minds in business.

Within 30 seconds, you know: this isn't theory. This is a laboratory notebook, and you're reading the results.

## Brand Voice: Honest Lab Notes
This post doesn't "sell" AI board members. It **reports** on them.

The voice is: analytical, slightly bemused, radically honest. We acknowledge the limitations. We show where AI falls short. We don't pretend these personas replace human judgment — we show what they ADD.

Think David Attenborough narrating the Serengeti, not a SaaS founder hyping features. Observant. Curious. Unafraid of complexity.

**Example tone:**
"Jensen never met a moat question he didn't like. Eleven reviews. Eleven variations on 'What stops competitors from copying this?' It's almost comical — until you realize he's caught three fatal flaws in products we almost shipped."

## What to Say NO To
**NO to generalities.** Every claim must be tied to a specific quote, a specific review, a specific moment of disagreement.

**NO to selling.** This is not a case study titled "How Great Minds AI Board Saved Our Startup." It's an honest accounting. If Warren missed something obvious, we say it.

**NO to personas speaking in unison.** The magic is in the COLLISION. If they all agree, that's boring. Show the fights. Show where Jensen's tech paranoia clashes with Oprah's emotional instincts.

**NO to academic language.** "Aggregate findings" becomes "patterns." "Synthesis of insights" becomes "what they caught." Write like you're explaining this to a founder at 2am after a hard pivot.

**NO to timidity.** Don't hedge with "potentially" or "in some cases." These AI personas are opinionated as hell. The post should match their energy.

## The Emotional Hook: You're Not Crazy
Here's why people will LOVE this:

**Founders doubt themselves constantly.** Is my idea good? Am I missing something fatal? Should I pivot?

This post says: "Here's what happens when you run your idea past Jensen, Warren, Shonda, and Oprah. Here's what they catch. Here's what they miss. Here's when they fight."

It's not validation. It's not a magic answer. It's **pattern recognition** — and patterns are how you develop taste.

The emotional core: **You're not alone in your doubts. Even the best ideas get shredded. The question is: are you listening to the right critiques?**

By the end, the reader should think: "I need to run MY idea through this gauntlet."

Not because it'll give them answers — but because it'll give them the RIGHT QUESTIONS.

---

**Final note:** This post is a Trojan Horse. It LOOKS like thought leadership about AI. But it's actually a demonstration of judgment — Great Minds' judgment about what makes products succeed.

The personas are the actors. The patterns are the script. But the director is us.

And people don't buy tools. They buy **taste**.
