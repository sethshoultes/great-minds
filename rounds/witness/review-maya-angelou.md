# Copy Review — Narrate

*Reviewed by Maya Angelou*

---

## The Soul of the Thing

Child, this tool has good bones. It knows what it wants to be: the quiet witness to your work, turning code into story. That's a beautiful intention. Now let's see if the words rise to meet it.

---

## Does the Language Feel Human?

Mostly, yes. "Your code, in plain English" — that's clean. It doesn't shout. It invites.

But then we stumble into "Safe and idempotent" — and suddenly we're in a board meeting. The heart goes cold. Technical people wrote this for technical people, and they forgot that even technical people are human beings first. We want to *feel* safe, not be *told* the word "idempotent."

The offline mode section says "It never produces file lists — always natural language." That's self-aware. That's good. The tool knows what it's against.

---

## Is There Rhythm in the Sentences?

Some. Listen to this:

> "You commit code normally. The post-commit hook fires. The process reads the diff. Claude Haiku generates a summary. The entry appears."

That's five short sentences, each one a beat. It moves like footsteps. That's rhythm.

But then:

> "Narrate is a CLI tool that auto-writes a plain-English changelog entry for every git commit."

That sentence is trying to carry too much furniture. It stumbles under its own weight. Twenty-three words before we can breathe.

---

## Does the Headline Stop You?

"Your code, in plain English."

It stops me. It makes a promise. Six words, and I understand exactly what I'm getting. That's rare. That's a gift.

But "Narrate is watching" — said after installation — that's trying to be clever and landing somewhere between reassuring and surveillance. The word "watching" has shadows in it now. Be careful what shadows you invite.

---

## Is Anything Trying Too Hard?

"No more cryptic commit histories. No effort required."

That second sentence is trying too hard. "No effort required" sounds like a late-night infomercial. It cheapens what comes before it. The first sentence earns its place. The second begs.

Also: "That is it." — written as three words instead of "That's it." It creates distance where intimacy was building. We were having a conversation, and suddenly you stepped behind a podium.

---

## The Three Weakest Lines (and What They Could Be)

### 1. Original:
> "Narrate is a CLI tool that auto-writes a plain-English changelog entry for every git commit."

**The problem:** It's a job description, not an introduction. All function, no feeling.

**Rewritten:**
> "Every commit tells a story. Narrate writes it down — one plain sentence, automatically, so you never have to."

---

### 2. Original:
> "No more cryptic commit histories. No effort required."

**The problem:** The first sentence opens a door. The second slams it shut with a sales pitch.

**Rewritten:**
> "No more cryptic commit histories. Just clarity, waiting for you."

---

### 3. Original:
> "Safe and idempotent — if a post-commit hook already exists, Narrate appends rather than overwriting."

**The problem:** "Idempotent" is a word for machines, not for people. And this sentence tells me *how* before it tells me *why I should care*.

**Rewritten:**
> "If you already have a post-commit hook, don't worry — Narrate adds itself gently, without disturbing what's there."

---

## Final Reflection

The copy knows what it wants to say. But sometimes it forgets who it's saying it to.

Remember: people won't remember your feature list. They'll remember how it felt to read about your tool and think, *"Finally. Someone made this for me."*

Speak to that person. They're tired. They've been staring at code all day. Give them one sentence that feels like a glass of cool water.

You're almost there.
