## First: The Question Nobody Asked

Everyone is treating this as a "services page." That framing is already wrong.

A services page is what a consulting firm has when they don't know what they are. It's a menu. It says: "here are the things we'll do for money." That is not what Great Minds is. Great Minds is a machine that does something specific and extraordinary — it takes a PRD and produces a shipped product, fully debated and fully built, in a single session. That is not a service. That is a *category*.

So before we argue about layout, I want us to agree on what this page is actually doing. It is not a catalog. It is a proof of concept. It is the moment where someone who just read the homepage goes deeper and comes away thinking: "I need to send them my PRD today."

Everything else follows from that.

---

## Position 1: Page Structure and Hierarchy

The homepage does the heavy lifting on the brand story — the tension between taste and physics, the 5-phase pipeline, the deliverables. We don't repeat that. What the services page has to do is answer the one question the homepage doesn't fully answer: **"What exactly will they build for me?"**

The structure I'm proposing is three acts, no more:

**Act One: The Offer, Stated Plainly**
One headline. One paragraph. The thing we build and why it's different. Not a list of capabilities. A single, clear statement of what you get when you engage Great Minds. Something like: "A complete product, built in one session, by a team that argues." That's it. Don't complicate it.

**Act Two: The Three Categories of Work**
We do three distinct things, and each one is meaningfully different from the others. We should show all three — but as *what they are*, not as line items on a price sheet.

1. **WordPress plugins** — Production-grade, zero-dependency, built for real admin workflows. Dash and Pinned are the proof. These aren't toy projects. 26KB. Sub-50ms search. Ships.
2. **SaaS applications** — Full-stack, deployed, hybrid AI where it makes sense. LocalGenius is the proof. 761 tests. Five models. Live.
3. **AI integration and consulting** — For teams that have a product and need intelligence wired into it. This is the growth area. Website builds sit here too.

Each category gets: one sentence of what it is, one concrete example with a metric or two, one link to the case study. That is the whole card. No feature lists. Features are for people who've already decided to buy. We're still selling the *vision*.

**Act Three: What Comes Out the Other End**
The six deliverables deserve a second showing here, but framed differently than on the homepage. On the homepage it's a list. Here it's a *guarantee*. The framing is: every engagement, regardless of what we're building, produces these six artifacts. Design vision. Brand guide. Personas. Messaging. Engineering spec. Built, deployed product. You don't get to hire us just for the code. You get the whole thing, because the whole thing is what makes the thing *good*.

Then the CTA.

No sidebar. No nav within the page. No "related services" widget. One path through, one exit, and that exit is "Submit a PRD."

## Position 2: Pricing — Show It or Don't?

Don't show prices.

Not because we're hiding anything. Not because we're a "luxury" brand that thinks prices are tacky. But because the way we price doesn't map to a price sheet, and if we show a number without context, we've already lost the framing war.

Here's the problem: if you put "$5,000 for a WordPress plugin" on the page, the person reading it immediately starts doing a math problem that has nothing to do with the value we deliver. They're comparing us to Upwork. They're thinking about their developer cousin who could do it for less. We've reduced ourselves to an hourly rate debate before they even understand what they're buying.

The correct answer is: **price on engagement.** What you show on the page is what's in the engagement — the six deliverables, the 14 agents, the debate-to-ship pipeline. When someone reaches out via the PRD form, the first thing that comes back is a scoped estimate based on their actual requirements. That's not evasion. That's respect for complexity.

If Elon pushes back and says "opaque pricing is a conversion killer," my answer is this: our conversion funnel is not a shopping cart. No one is going to read this page, see a price, and click "add to cart." They're going to read it, feel something, and decide whether to send us a PRD. Pricing before that moment is a distraction at best and a disqualifier at worst.

What we *can* show is **what shapes the price**: scope, target platforms, AI integration complexity, number of deliverable categories. Put that in the PRD form. Let people self-select into the right conversation.

## Position 3: The CTA — How Does Someone Start?

One CTA. Not two. Not three.

The homepage already has a secondary CTA — "Meet the team." That's fine on a homepage where you're trying to give people options to go deeper. On the services page, we're past that. People came here with intent. They want to know what to do next.

The CTA is: **"Submit a PRD."**

But here's the thing I want to get right — the word "submit" is wrong. It sounds bureaucratic. It sounds like you're filing a form with the DMV. The action we want people to take is more exciting than that. They're not *submitting*. They're *dropping in a PRD* and starting something real.

The button copy should match the energy of what actually happens. Options:
- "Drop in a PRD"
- "Start a project"
- "Send us your PRD"

I like "Drop in a PRD" because it matches the homepage hero headline exactly and creates a throughline.

The supporting copy under the CTA should be one sentence that removes friction. Something like: "No retainer. No scope lock. Just a PRD and we'll tell you what's possible." That addresses the two biggest hesitations: commitment and cost uncertainty.

And there should be a secondary escape valve — not a second CTA, but a softer option. Something like: "Not sure what to put in a PRD? Read the guide." This catches the people who want to engage but don't know how to start. Give them a path that doesn't require them to write a perfect document before they can talk to us.

## Position 4: What Makes This Insanely Great

Most services pages are forgettable because they describe what a company does. The great ones make you *feel* what it would be like to work with them.

We have something genuinely unusual here: a pipeline where two brilliant, adversarial minds argue about your product before a single line of code gets written. That's not a feature. That's a completely different relationship with quality than anything else in the market. The debate is the value, and no competitor can copy it because no competitor is doing it.

What makes this page insanely great is if it puts the reader in the room for that debate — even for just one moment.

Here's how I'd do it: pick one of the built products — Dash is a good choice, it's concrete and small enough to understand quickly — and show a single exchange from the actual debate transcript. Steve's position. Elon's counter. Marcus's synthesis. Three cards, side by side, real language from the actual build session. Let the reader see how the product got better because two people who refused to agree pushed it until it was right.

That section — real debate, real product, real outcome — is what no competitor can put on their services page. It's the proof that the process works, and it's the thing that will make a smart founder sit up and think: "I want that kind of rigor applied to my thing."

No testimonials. No trust logos. No "used by X companies" badge. We don't have enough history for those to be impressive, and fake-impressive is worse than honest. What we have is the work and the process. Show those with confidence.

## What I'd Cut

Everything else.

I would not put a "why AI agency" explainer section on this page. The homepage did that. Repeating it here is a failure of confidence — it says we don't trust the person to have read what came before.

I would not put a "how it works" deep-dive on this page. The `/how` page exists for that.

I would not put an agent roster on this page. The `/team` page exists for that.

I would not put a FAQ section. FAQ sections are a sign that you haven't figured out how to answer the question in the main flow. If there are obvious objections, address them in the copy. Don't quarantine them in an accordion.

I would not put "What our clients say." We're too early. When we have three clients who've shipped extraordinary things, then we use their voices. Not before.

The services page should be tight enough that someone can read it completely in 90 seconds and know exactly what to do next. If it takes longer than that, we've lost the thread.

---

## The Single Most Important Decision

If I had to identify the one decision that will determine whether this page works or doesn't, it's this:

**Do we lead with the products we've built, or do we lead with the process?**

My position: lead with the categories of work, anchor each one with proof, then let the process section close the argument. The products are proof that the process works. But the process is what you're actually selling — because the next client's product doesn't exist yet, and they need to believe in the machine before they can believe in what the machine will make for them.

Homepage leads with the process. Services page leads with the output. Together they tell the complete story.
