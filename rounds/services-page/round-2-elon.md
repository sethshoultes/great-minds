## Challenge 1: The "Category" Framing — Steve is Wrong, But Not Completely

Steve says treating this as a "services page" is wrong framing. He says Great Minds is a *category*, not a menu. And then — here's where he loses me — he proceeds to describe... three categories of work. With cards. With case study links. Which is a services page.

The disagreement isn't about framing. It's about taxonomy.

Steve's three categories are organized by *output type*: WordPress plugins, SaaS apps, AI consulting. Mine are organized by *buyer intent*: buy something we built, hire us to build something, buy access to our process.

**Steve's taxonomy is internal. Mine is external.**

When a founder lands on this page, they are not thinking "I need a WordPress plugin." They are thinking "I have this PRD for a product and I need to know if you can build it and how much it costs." The question in their head is about the relationship, not the technology.

My three-category model — Products, Builds, Pipeline-as-a-Service — maps to the three reasons someone shows up to an agency website. Steve's model maps to what's in our portfolio. Those are different things.

**I'm keeping my taxonomy. Steve should adopt it.**

However — and this is where Steve is right — each category should be anchored with proof. Dash for Products, LocalGenius for Builds, the pipeline session itself for Pipeline-as-a-Service. That synthesis I accept. The category labels are mine; the "proof point anchoring" approach is Steve's and it's correct.

## Challenge 2: Pricing — This Is the Biggest Disagreement and I Won't Back Down

Steve says: don't show prices. The framing war matters more than the number.

I say: hiding prices is a product manager's mistake dressed up as brand strategy.

Let me steelman Steve's position first, because it's not crazy. He's right that if you show "$5,000 for a WordPress plugin" without context, you've already entered the wrong debate. He's right that this isn't a shopping cart. He's right that premature price comparison against Upwork is a losing game.

Here's where he's wrong: the alternative isn't "show nothing." The alternative is "show anchors that reframe the math."

Steve's solution — "price on engagement, show what shapes the price" — is still opaque. It's asking someone to fill out a form before they know if they're in the right order of magnitude. That's friction. That filters out exactly the kind of self-sufficient founder who reads a PRD, knows what they want, and just needs to know if you're in their budget universe.

The physics of conversion funnels: every additional step before a buyer gets a number reduces conversion by roughly 20-30%. That's not a guess — it's documented across thousands of SaaS and services funnels. Asking someone to "submit a PRD to find out if you can afford us" is asking them to invest before you've given them reason to believe the ROI exists.

**My position:** Show price floors. Not ceilings. Not ranges. Floors.

- "Builds start at $X" — anchors the conversation without committing to scope
- "Pipeline sessions from $X" — signals you're not Upwork, not McKinsey
- Products: just show the actual price, it's a product

"Projects start at $5,000" does three things simultaneously: (1) filters out people who were never going to hire us anyway, (2) signals quality and seriousness to buyers in budget, (3) removes the "are they expensive?" anxiety that prevents form submissions.

Steve says showing a number triggers comparison to their developer cousin. I say: anyone who compares a 14-agent pipeline that ships a full product in one session to their developer cousin was never going to hire us regardless of what we put on the page. Stop designing for the buyer you can't convert.

**Locked: Price floors shown. Not full pricing. Floors.**

## Challenge 3: The Debate Transcript — Brilliant Idea, Wrong Placement

Steve wants to show a real debate excerpt — Steve's position, Elon's counter, Marcus's synthesis — three cards, side by side. He says this is "what no competitor can put on their services page."

He's 100% right that it's differentiated. He's wrong about where it belongs.

Here's the problem with putting a debate transcript on the services page: the person reading the services page is already fairly sold. They came from the homepage. They have intent. What they need at this stage is *confidence*, not *discovery*. The debate transcript is a discovery experience — it explains the process to someone who doesn't already understand it.

The debate transcript belongs on the homepage as the "how we're different" proof section. OR it belongs on the `/how` page as the centerpiece. On the services page it is:
- Increasing time-on-page for the wrong reason (entertainment vs. conversion)
- Competing with the CTA for attention
- Adding length to a page that should be scannable in 90 seconds (Steve's own metric)

What I'd put on the services page instead: **metrics from the debate outcomes**. Not transcripts. Numbers. "Debate sessions catch an average of X critical product decisions before a line of code is written." Or: "The gap between Steve's first recommendation and the final spec averages Y%. That gap is where most projects fail." That's the debate's value in two sentences, which fits a services page.

**Position: Real debate transcript → homepage or /how. Services page gets debate metrics.**

However — and this is important — Steve's instinct that we need a "proof of process" moment on the services page is correct. The delivery mechanism I'm rejecting, not the intent. I'll keep the intent.

## Challenge 5: Page Length — Steve's 90-Second Rule vs. My "Close Fast" Instinct

Steve says: "The services page should be tight enough that someone can read it completely in 90 seconds."

I said: minimum viable pitch, ship fast, iterate.

We're aligned on the outcome but I want to be precise about what that means in pixels and sections:

90 seconds of reading at average pace (200-250 words/minute) is roughly 300-375 words of body copy. That is very short. It means:
- No intro paragraph that restates the homepage
- No "we believe" statements
- No process deep-dive
- No long case study summaries

What fits in 300 words of body copy: three category names with one-sentence descriptions each, three proof metrics, six deliverable names (not descriptions), one CTA with two supporting lines.

That's the whole page. Everything else is a linked deep-dive.

**Aligned. Sub-400 words of body copy. Everything else is a link.**

## The 3 Decisions That Are Now Locked

After two rounds, these three decisions are no longer up for debate. We build to these.

### LOCKED DECISION 1: Taxonomy
**We use buyer-intent taxonomy, not output taxonomy.**

Three categories:
- **Products** — things we built that you buy (Dash, Pinned, LocalGenius pricing pages)
- **Builds** — we build your PRD (websites, SaaS, AI integrations)
- **Pipeline-as-a-Service** — the full 14-agent debate-to-ship session, run against your PRD

Each category: one-sentence description, one proof-point (existing product or metric), one price anchor, one CTA link.

This is not negotiable. Organizing by output type (WordPress plugins, SaaS, AI consulting) is org-chart thinking. We organize by buyer intent.

---

### LOCKED DECISION 2: Price Transparency
**We show price floors. Not full pricing tables. Floors.**

- Products: actual prices (they're products, price them like products)
- Builds: "Projects start at $X" — floor only, full scope on engagement
- Pipeline-as-a-Service: "Sessions from $X" — floor, with link to full pricing tier page

We do NOT do "contact us for pricing." We do NOT do "price on engagement" with no anchor. We give buyers enough to self-qualify.

The argument against this is dead. The conversion math is unambiguous. Show a number.

---

### LOCKED DECISION 3: Page Constraint
**The services page is a conversion page, not a storytelling page.**

Rules:
- Under 400 words of body copy
- No debate transcript (that goes on homepage or /how)
- No agent roster, no FAQ, no "why AI agency" explainer
- Process shown as five compressed steps, max 8 words each, links to /how
- One primary CTA: "Drop in a PRD"
- One secondary escape valve: "Not sure what to put in a PRD? Read the guide"
- No "used by X companies," no testimonials (we don't have strong ones yet)

What this page does in one sentence: confirms you're in the right place, shows you what you get, gives you a price floor, tells you exactly what to do next.
