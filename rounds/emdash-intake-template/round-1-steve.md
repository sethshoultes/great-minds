# Steve Jobs — Round 1 Positions

## Product Naming

**Call it "Spark."**

Not "Emdash Intake Template." That's a feature description, not a name. Spark. One syllable. It's the moment before the fire — the instant you have an idea and need it real. "I Sparked my site." "Just Spark it." The verb writes itself.

Emdash is the parent brand. Spark is the product. When someone asks "How'd you build this?" the answer should be "Spark" — not "the Emdash intake template system."

---

## Design Philosophy

This is the anti-Squarespace.

Every website builder in history has said "Here are the tools — go build something." We're saying "Tell us what you need — we already built it." The insanely great part isn't the technology. It's the *absence* of decisions.

Five questions. That's not a limitation — that's the product. Every question we removed is a gift to the customer. We did the work so they don't have to.

The design philosophy is **inference over input**. We don't ask for colors. We *see* their photos and *know* their industry and *feel* the right palette. That's taste at scale.

---

## User Experience — The First 30 Seconds

The first 30 seconds should feel like talking to a friend who happens to be a designer.

Not a form. Not a wizard. A conversation. "What's this for?" — like a bartender asking what kind of night you're having. The customer types one sentence. That's it. We're already building.

By the time they've answered three questions, they should start wondering *when does the hard part start?* The answer is: it doesn't. That's the magic. The moment of delight is when they realize there's no "next step" — there's just a live website.

---

## Brand Voice

Confident. Warm. Zero jargon.

We speak like the best creative director you ever worked with: someone who *gets it* instantly and never makes you feel dumb for not knowing CSS. We never say "optimize your digital presence" or "leverage your brand assets." We say "What makes you different?" and "Upload what you have."

The voice is your brilliant friend who builds websites on the weekend and insists on doing yours for free because they genuinely want to help.

---

## What to Say NO To

**No CMS.** The moment someone logs into a dashboard, we've failed. Revisions happen in conversation.

**No templates gallery.** Choosing a template is asking the customer to do our job. We choose. They approve.

**No pricing page (yet).** Ship the experience first. Make it undeniable. Then figure out how to charge for it.

**No "advanced options."** The power users aren't our customers. The overwhelmed business owner is. Every feature that serves the 5% expert betrays the 95% human.

**No onboarding tutorial.** If it needs a tutorial, it's broken.

---

## The Emotional Hook

People will love this because **it respects their time and trusts their words.**

Every other tool treats customers like they need to learn something before they can have something. We treat them like experts in their own business — which they are. They know what they do. They know what makes them special. They just don't know how to turn that into a website.

We do.

The emotional hook is: *"Someone finally listened to me — and actually did something with it."*

That's the feeling. That's what they'll tell their friends. That's why they'll come back.

*That's not a feature. That's a revolution.*
