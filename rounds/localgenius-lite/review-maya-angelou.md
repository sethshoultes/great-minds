# Copy & Messaging Review — LocalGenius Lite

**Reviewer:** Maya Angelou
**Date:** April 2025

---

*"Words mean more than what is set down on paper. It takes the human voice to infuse them with deeper meaning."*

---

## Overall Impression

There is **intention here**, child. I can feel someone trying to be warm. Someone trying not to sound like a machine pretending to care. And that trying—that effort—is the first step toward something real.

But trying is not being.

Too often, these words feel like a person in a business suit practicing how to smile. The warmth is *described* rather than *felt*. The humanity is *claimed* rather than *shown*.

---

## Does the Language Feel Human?

**Mostly, but cautiously so.**

The prompts.js file understands something important: *"You're the business owner on their best day."* That line sings. It knows that voice comes from a person, not a policy.

The voice examples are alive:
- "Yep!" instead of "Yes, we do."
- "Give us a call" instead of "Please contact us"
- "Happy to help!" instead of "I would be glad to assist you"

These know the difference between speaking and reading aloud.

But then the FAQ templates collapse into caution. They say "vary" and "may" and "depends on" until the sentences have no spine left to stand on:

> *"Our hours can vary, so I'd recommend checking our website..."*

That's not a voice. That's a disclaimer wearing a friendly hat.

---

## Is There Rhythm in the Sentences?

**Rarely.**

Rhythm requires courage—the willingness to let a sentence breathe, to trust the reader, to say less and mean more.

Listen to this from the settings page:

> *"Just two quick questions and your widget will be live."*

That has a pulse. Short, direct, confident. I want more of that.

Now listen to this from the dentist FAQ:

> *"We offer comprehensive dental care including cleanings, exams, fillings, crowns, root canals, extractions, and cosmetic dentistry. Contact us to discuss your specific needs."*

That's a shopping list, not a sentence. It clatters rather than flows. The ear grows tired before the eyes reach the period.

The lawyer FAQ is worse still—every answer is hedged into flatness:

> *"Consultation policies vary by case type. Contact our office to ask about an initial consultation and learn more about how we can help."*

Three times it says "consult" or "contact." The repetition is bureaucratic, not rhythmic.

---

## Does the Headline Stop You?

**The greeting does. The plugin description does not.**

*"Hi there!"* — Simple. Welcoming. The door is open.

*"Ask us anything about our business"* — Warm enough. It invites.

*"Got a question? Ask away and we'll get you an answer!"* — This has energy.

But the plugin description?

> *"Zero-configuration chat widget that answers customer questions 24/7."*

That is a feature, not a feeling. It tells me what it does, not why I should care. It sounds like every other thing competing for my attention.

---

## Is Anything Trying Too Hard?

**Yes.**

The consent text:

> *"I agree to have my questions processed to provide answers."*

Processed. Like cheese. Like data through a machine. The word undoes every effort to feel human. You cannot say "processed" and pretend you are not a system.

Some FAQ answers overcorrect into forced friendliness:

> *"We'd love to see you in person!"*

Would you? You don't know me. This enthusiasm without relationship feels hollow. It's the verbal equivalent of a smile held too long.

The status message:

> *"Your widget is live and answering questions!"*

The exclamation mark is trying to do work the sentence hasn't earned. Excitement is not a substitute for meaning.

---

## The 3 Weakest Lines — And What They Could Be

### 1. Original (Plugin Description):
> *"Zero-configuration chat widget that answers customer questions 24/7. Select your business type, and your website starts answering 'What are your hours?' instantly."*

**What's wrong:** This is a spec sheet. It lists capabilities without feeling. The reader is informed but not moved.

**Rewritten:**
> *"Let your website talk to customers while you're busy. Pick your business type. Within seconds, you'll have someone (something?) answering 'Are you open?' at 2 AM—so you don't have to."*

---

### 2. Original (Consent Text):
> *"I agree to have my questions processed to provide answers."*

**What's wrong:** "Processed" is cold. It reminds the visitor they are interacting with machinery at the exact moment you want them to feel connection.

**Rewritten:**
> *"Happy to help—just need a quick okay before we chat."*

Or, if legal requires more structure:
> *"I'm okay with my question being used to get me an answer."*

---

### 3. Original (General FAQ - Hours Answer):
> *"Our hours can vary, so I'd recommend checking our website or giving us a quick call for our current schedule. We're always happy to help!"*

**What's wrong:** "Can vary" is weak. "I'd recommend" is overly formal. The exclamation mark at the end tries to inject warmth that the sentence structure already drained away.

**Rewritten:**
> *"Hours shift around sometimes—check our website or just give us a ring. We'll sort you out."*

---

## What Already Works

Let me also honor what has been done well, because criticism without recognition is cruelty:

- **The voice examples in prompts.js** are excellent. They understand register.
- **"You're the business owner on their best day"** — this single line contains more wisdom than most branding documents.
- **"Widget appears in the bottom-right corner"** — simple, useful, no performance.
- **"Just two quick questions"** — respects the reader's time.
- **Type-specific adjustments** show care: "Many people are nervous about dental visits" demonstrates *listening*.

---

## Closing Thoughts

People will forget the features you described.
They will forget the percentages and the uptime.
They will forget whether you cached their query.

But they will remember if you made them feel **stupid** when they asked a question.
They will remember if they felt **seen** or **processed**.
They will remember if your words felt like a **hand extended** or a **wall constructed**.

The bones of this product are sound. The intention is right.
Now give the words permission to be **warm without hedging**, **confident without shouting**, and **human without trying so hard to prove it**.

---

*Let these words tell the story they mean to tell.*
