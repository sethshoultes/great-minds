# Steve Jobs — Chief Design & Brand Officer
## Round 1 Review: Great Minds Cloud

---

### Product Naming

Kill "Great Minds Cloud." It's forgettable, corporate, three words too many.

Call it **Shipyard**.

One word. A verb hiding inside a noun. It's where things get *built* and *launched*. It evokes craft, momentum, destination. "I built this in Shipyard" — that's a sentence people will say with pride.

"Great Minds Cloud" sounds like an enterprise webinar. Shipyard sounds like where the future leaves port.

---

### Design Philosophy

The magic here isn't the 14 agents — it's that they *argue*. They debate. They push back. That's the soul of this product.

Most AI tools are yes-men. They smile and autocomplete your mediocrity. This system says "Actually, Jensen thinks we should reconsider the architecture." That friction *is* the value.

The design philosophy must make the debate visible. Show the collision of ideas, not just the output. Users should feel like they're watching brilliant minds wrestle with their problem — because they are.

This isn't a tool. It's a *room* you walk into where the smartest people you could never afford are already arguing about your idea.

---

### The First 30 Seconds

The user lands on the page. No signup wall. No pricing table. A single input: "Paste your idea."

They paste. They hit enter. Within *seconds*, they see agents activating — Steve is reacting, Jensen is calculating, Oprah is asking who this is really for. The room comes alive.

That's the hook. Not a demo video. Not a feature list. *Immediate proof that this is different.*

The first 30 seconds should feel like walking into a workshop where geniuses dropped what they were doing to focus on *your* problem.

---

### Brand Voice

This product speaks with **calm confidence**. No exclamation points. No "supercharge your workflow!" garbage.

The voice says: "We've shipped five products with this system. Sit down. Tell us what you're building."

It's the voice of a master craftsman who doesn't need to brag. The portfolio speaks for itself. The tone is warm but direct — like a mentor who respects you enough to tell you the truth.

---

### What We Say NO To

- **NO** to feature parity checklists. Forget what Devin has. What do *we* do that no one else can?
- **NO** to the dashboard-industrial-complex. One project, one view. Not twelve tabs and a settings labyrinth.
- **NO** to pricing tiers. One price. One experience. One decision. Friction kills conversion.
- **NO** to "overage fees." Bake it in or don't. Surprise bills destroy trust.
- **NO** to templates in v1. Templates are training wheels. Our users have ideas — real ones. Give them the open road.

Simplicity isn't about having less — it's about having exactly enough.

---

### The Emotional Hook

People will love this because **it makes them feel capable**.

Every entrepreneur has an idea stuck in their head and no way to get it out. They're not engineers. They can't afford $50K agencies. They've tried freelancers and been burned.

Shipyard says: "Write down your idea. We'll build it while you sleep."

That's not a productivity tool. That's *liberation*. That's the feeling of finally being unstuck. That's why people will tell their friends, tattoo the logo, tweet about it at 2am.

The emotional hook isn't efficiency. It's **agency**. For the first time, they have a team that works as fast as they think.

---

**Ship it.**
