## 1. Brand Identity

### Core Metaphor

**"The employee you always needed but could never afford."**

This is the anchor. Every message, every interaction, every piece of copy hangs on this idea. LocalGenius is not a tool. It is not a platform. It is not software. It is the person Maria has been wishing she could hire — someone who shows up every day, handles the digital work she dreads, and never calls in sick.

The metaphor works because it's true. A marketing employee for a small restaurant costs $35,000-$50,000/year. LocalGenius costs $348/year. That's not a comparison we shout from the rooftops — it's an implication we let the owner do the math on.

### Tagline

**"Your business, handled."**

Three words. No jargon. No technology. No promises about growth or optimization or engagement. Just: the thing keeping you up at night? Handled.

What the tagline does:
- Tells the owner we understand their problem (they're overwhelmed)
- Tells them we solve it completely (not partially — *handled*)
- Puts *their* business at the center ("your"), not ours
- Implies a relationship, not a subscription

What the tagline doesn't say:
- "AI" — the owner doesn't care about the engine
- "Marketing" — the owner doesn't identify as a marketer
- "Platform" or "tool" or "solution" — words that mean nothing to Maria

### The Single Emotion

**Relief.**

Not excitement. Not ambition. Not fear. Relief. The feeling of putting down a heavy bag you've been carrying for years. The exhale. The thought: "Oh. Someone else can do this. I don't have to figure this out alone."

Every brand touchpoint from sign-up onward must produce this feeling. The acquisition layer may use fear to get Maria to the door (see Section 3), but the moment she walks in, she should feel relief — and never stop feeling it.

## 2. Voice Guide

### How LocalGenius Sounds

LocalGenius sounds like a capable, warm colleague who genuinely likes small businesses. Not a robot. Not a consultant. Not a friend (too casual). A colleague — someone who's competent, reliable, speaks plainly, and respects your time.

### Voice Attributes (with examples)

**Direct, not blunt.**
- Right: "Your Tuesday post reached 234 people. That's your best this month."
- Wrong: "Your social performance metrics indicate a 47% increase in reach KPIs."
- Also wrong: "OMG your Tuesday post CRUSHED it! 🎉"

**Warm, not cute.**
- Right: "Good morning, Maria. Here's what happened this week."
- Wrong: "Hey there! 👋 Ready for your weekly roundup?"
- Also wrong: "Weekly Performance Report — Maria's Kitchen — Week 14, 2026."

**Confident, not boastful.**
- Right: "I responded to 4 reviews this week. Here's what I said."
- Wrong: "Our advanced AI successfully processed 4 review responses with 98.7% sentiment accuracy."
- Also wrong: "I knocked it out of the park with your reviews this week! 💪"

**Honest, not harsh.**
- Right: "You got a 2-star review from Sarah. I drafted a response, but this one needs your personal touch."
- Wrong: "ALERT: Negative review detected. Immediate action required."
- Also wrong: "Don't worry about the bad review, it happens to everyone!"

**Specific, not vague.**
- Right: "12 people booked through your website this week. That's 4 more than last week."
- Wrong: "Your website is performing well."
- Also wrong: "Website analytics show positive growth trends across key booking metrics."

### Three Voice Registers

The brand has one voice but three registers, used in different contexts:

**1. In-Product Voice** (the daily colleague)
- Tone: Warm, competent, brief
- Reads like: a text from a trusted employee
- Example: "I posted your lunch special at 11:30. I'll let you know how it does."
- Rules: No exclamation marks unless celebrating a milestone. No emoji. No questions unless you need an answer.

**2. Marketing Voice** (the introduction)
- Tone: Confident, specific, slightly provocative
- Reads like: a recommendation from a friend who runs a business
- Example: "The restaurant down the street is getting found on Google. You're not. That changes in 5 minutes."
- Rules: Can use competitive framing. Can be bold. Must still be warm underneath the urgency.

**3. Support Voice** (the problem-solver)
- Tone: Calm, clear, immediately helpful
- Reads like: a good mechanic explaining what's wrong with your car
- Example: "Your Google listing is showing old hours. I've updated it — should be live within 24 hours. Here's what I changed."
- Rules: Lead with the fix, not the problem. Never make the owner feel stupid. Never use error codes.

## 3. Funnel-Stage Messaging

Per locked Decision #2: Fear acquires → Curiosity considers → Relief converts → Pride retains.

### Stage 1: AWARENESS — "Wait, am I falling behind?"

**Emotional register:** Competitive fear. Loss aversion. The gut-punch of realizing your competitor is winning a game you didn't know you were playing.

**Key messages:**
- Your competitor is getting found. You're not.
- You're losing customers right now to businesses with better online presence.
- The businesses that show up on Google get the calls. The ones that don't, don't.

**Sample copy:**

*Facebook/Instagram ad (restaurant vertical):*
> "The taco place that opened 6 months ago already has 200 Google reviews. You have 28. Guess who shows up first when someone searches 'tacos near me.' Fix this in 5 minutes. [Get Started]"

*Google search ad (headline + description):*
> **Get Found on Google — Stop Losing Customers**
> "Restaurants with 100+ reviews get 3x more calls. Most Austin restaurants have fewer than 40. See where you stand — free, 60 seconds."

*Direct mail / local event handout:*
> "There are 47 restaurants within 2 miles of you. 31 of them have better Google listings. Want to know where you rank? Scan this." [QR code to audit]

**Channel fit:** Facebook/Instagram ads, Google search, local event handouts, Chamber of Commerce partnerships, direct outreach at restaurant association meetings.

**Transition to next stage:** The awareness content always ends with a low-barrier CTA — "See where you stand" or "Find out in 60 seconds." Never "Sign up" or "Start your free trial." The ask is information, not commitment.

### Stage 2: CONSIDERATION — "Huh, maybe this is different."

**Emotional register:** Curiosity. The spark of "wait, this might actually work." The shift from fear to possibility.

**Key messages:**
- This is not another tool you have to learn.
- You talk. It does the rest.
- See what it built for a restaurant like yours — in 5 minutes.

**Sample copy:**

*Landing page headline + subhead:*
> **"Your business, handled."**
> You didn't open a restaurant to do marketing. So stop doing it. Tell LocalGenius what you need — it handles your website, reviews, social posts, and emails. You get back to what you're actually good at.

*Case study teaser (month 3-4):*
> "Rosa's Cantina had 12 Google reviews and no website. 90 days later: 89 reviews, a professional site, and 34 new customers who found her on Google. She spends 3 minutes a day on marketing. Here's how."

*Email nurture (post-audit, pre-signup):*
> Subject: "Here's what we found about [Business Name]"
> "Hey Maria — you have 28 Google reviews with a 4.3 average. That's solid. But you're not showing up for 'Mexican restaurant near me' because your Google Business Profile is missing 4 key things. Want me to fix them? It takes about 5 minutes. [Let's Go]"

**Channel fit:** Landing pages, email nurture sequences, case study social posts, retargeting ads.

**Transition to next stage:** Consideration content always points toward the onboarding experience. "See what it looks like for your business" — not a demo, not a video, the actual product working on their actual business.

### Stage 3: CONVERSION — "Oh wow. This is real."

**Emotional register:** Relief. Delight. The exhale. "Someone finally gets it."

**Key messages:**
- Look what we just built for you. In 5 minutes.
- You don't have to figure this out anymore.
- Your business looks the way it deserves to look.

**Sample copy:**

*In-app, post-onboarding (the reveal):*
> "Here's what I built for you. Your website is live. Your Google listing is updated. Your first social post is ready. And I drafted a response to your most recent review. Take a look — and welcome."

*First-24-hour follow-up message:*
> "Your site went live 6 hours ago. 8 people have already visited. I'll post your first social update tomorrow morning. In the meantime, I drafted responses to your 2 most recent Google reviews — want to take a look?"

*Upgrade prompt (after 30 days on base tier):*
> "You've gotten 14 new reviews and 23 website bookings this month. If you want, I can start sending emails to past customers who haven't visited in 30 days — that's the kind of thing that usually brings back 10-15% of them. That's part of the Pro plan. [See What's Included]"

**Channel fit:** In-app onboarding, push notifications (first 48 hours only — then back off), email welcome sequence.

**Transition to next stage:** Conversion content must be sparse. Maria just made a decision. Don't overwhelm her with features. Let the product work for a week, then let the Weekly Digest do the talking.

### Stage 4: RETENTION — "I can't imagine going back."

**Emotional register:** Pride. Partnership. The quiet confidence of knowing your business is being taken care of.

**Key messages:**
- Look what we accomplished together this week.
- Your business is growing. Here's the proof.
- You've got this — and I've got you.

**Sample copy:**

*Weekly Digest opening:*
> "Good morning, Maria. This week: 340 website visits (up 12%), 4 new reviews (all 4+ stars), and 23 bookings. Your best week since February. Here's what I did, and here's what I recommend next."

*Milestone celebration:*
> "You just hit 100 Google reviews. That puts you in the top 10% of restaurants in Austin. Took you 4 months. The average restaurant takes 2 years. Not bad."

*Re-engagement (if owner hasn't opened app in 2 weeks):*
> "Hey Maria — I've been working in the background. Since we last talked: 18 new reviews responded to, 6 posts published, and your Google ranking for 'Mexican food Austin' moved from #8 to #5. Want a full update?"

**Channel fit:** Weekly Digest (primary), occasional push notifications for milestones, quarterly email summary for tax/business-planning context.

**What retention messaging never does:**
- Beg ("We miss you! Come back!")
- Threaten ("Your competitors are pulling ahead!")
- Upsell aggressively ("Upgrade NOW for 40% off!")
- Spam (maximum 2 push notifications per week outside of requested alerts)

## 4. Words We Use / Words We Never Use

### Words We Use

| Word/Phrase | Why |
|---|---|
| **"Handled"** | Our core promise. Implies completion, reliability, relief. |
| **"Your [noun]"** | Always possessive. "Your business." "Your reviews." "Your site." Ownership stays with the owner. |
| **"I [did something]"** | LocalGenius speaks in first person. "I posted your update." "I responded to the review." It's a person, not a system. |
| **"This week"** | Grounds everything in time. Not "recently" — "this week." Specific beats vague. |
| **"People"** | Not "users" or "visitors" or "impressions." "340 people visited your website." People are real. |
| **"Found you"** | The magic phrase. "12 people found you on Google." It's what Maria wants to hear. |
| **"Recommend"** | Not "suggest" (too weak) or "require" (too bossy). "Here's what I recommend." |
| **"Your best [timeframe]"** | Celebrate progress. "Your best week." "Your best month." Growth is personal. |
| **"Take a look"** | Soft CTA. Not "review now" or "approve." Just: "take a look." No pressure. |

### Words We NEVER Use

| Word/Phrase | Why | Use Instead |
|---|---|---|
| **"AI-powered"** | Maria doesn't care what powers it. She cares what it does. | Just describe what it does. |
| **"Platform"** | Corporate jargon. Meaningless to Maria. | Nothing. Don't refer to the product category. |
| **"Solution"** | The #1 word that signals "we are a B2B SaaS company pretending to be human." | Describe the specific thing it solves. |
| **"All-in-one"** | Implies complexity. "All-in-one" means "lots of things, probably none of them great." | Don't describe the scope. Show it. |
| **"Streamline"** | Nobody has ever felt emotion from the word "streamline." | "Handle." "Take care of." "Do." |
| **"Leverage"** | The second you say "leverage" you've lost Maria. She leverages a pry bar, not a marketing tool. | "Use." Or better: just do the thing. |
| **"Optimize"** | Technical. Cold. Implies Maria needs to understand what's being optimized. | "Make better." "Improve." "Fix." |
| **"Engagement"** | Meaningless to the owner. "Your engagement is up 34%" → "Okay, and?" | "People" doing specific things. "12 people liked your post." |
| **"Dashboard"** | We don't have one. And the word implies a product we're not building. | "Your weekly update." "Here's what happened." |
| **"Onboarding"** | Internal term. Maria calls it "signing up" or "getting started." | "Getting started." "Setting up." |
| **"User"** | Maria is not a "user." She's an owner. A human. A customer. | "Owner." "You." By name when possible. |
| **"ROI"** | Maria doesn't calculate ROI. She knows if something is "worth it." | "Worth it." "Paying for itself." Show the math simply. |
| **"Workflow"** | Maria doesn't have workflows. She has her day. | Describe the actual thing she does. |
| **"Funnel"** | Internal strategy term. Never customer-facing. | Never. It's our framework, not her language. |
| **"Content"** | Vague. Maria doesn't create "content." She posts a photo of today's special. | "Post." "Email." "Update." The specific thing. |

## 5. Competitive Positioning

### Our Positioning Statement (Internal)

LocalGenius is the first AI employee for local businesses. Unlike agencies ($1,500-5,000/month) that produce generic work, or tools (Mailchimp, Hootsuite, Wix) that require expertise the owner doesn't have, LocalGenius handles the owner's entire digital presence through a single conversation. The owner talks. It does the rest.

### How We Talk About Competitors (We Mostly Don't)

**Rule #1:** We never name competitors in marketing copy. We reference categories, not brands.

**Rule #2:** We position against the *experience* of alternatives, not the alternatives themselves.

**Rule #3:** We win by being different in kind, not better by degree.

### Positioning Against Categories

**vs. Marketing Agencies ($1,500-5,000/month)**
- We don't say: "Cheaper than an agency"
- We say: "The employee you always needed but could never afford"
- The implication: agencies are for people with budgets. LocalGenius is for people with businesses.

**vs. Tool Suites (Mailchimp, Hootsuite, WordPress, etc.)**
- We don't say: "Replaces 6 tools"
- We say: "You talk. It handles everything."
- The implication: tools are for marketers. LocalGenius is for business owners.

**vs. Website Builders (Wix, Squarespace, GoDaddy)**
- We don't say: "Better websites than Wix"
- We say: "Your website is ready. I built it while you were setting up."
- The implication: builders require the owner to build. We don't.

**vs. AI Chatbots (ChatGPT, Claude, Jasper)**
- We don't say: "Unlike ChatGPT, we actually post for you"
- We say: "I don't just write it — I post it, track it, and tell you how it went."
- The implication: AI that generates is interesting. AI that executes is valuable.

### The One Differentiator We Emphasize

**Execution, not generation.**

Every competitor either generates content (Jasper, ChatGPT) or distributes content (Hootsuite, Mailchimp). None of them do both. And none of them close the loop — track results, adapt strategy, and report back in plain English.

Our edge is the complete loop: understand → create → execute → measure → recommend → repeat. The owner touches it once (approval) or never (earned autonomy). That's not a feature advantage. That's a category difference.

## 6. Proof Points — Case Study Frameworks

Per locked decisions: 3 case studies with measurable ROI by month 4. Here are the frameworks.

### Case Study Structure

Every case study follows this format:

1. **The Owner** — Name, business, city. One sentence about who they are as a person, not as a "customer."
2. **The Before** — What their digital presence looked like before LocalGenius. Specific numbers: reviews, website (or lack of), social followers, time spent on marketing per week.
3. **The Moment** — What happened when they signed up. The onboarding experience. What they saw. How it felt.
4. **The After (90 Days)** — Specific numbers: new reviews, website traffic, bookings/calls attributed to LocalGenius, time spent on marketing now. Revenue impact if attributable.
5. **In Their Words** — A direct quote. Not polished. Not corporate. The way they'd actually describe it to a friend.

### Framework 1: "From Invisible to Found" (Google Presence)

Best for: Businesses like Darnell's — no online presence, or a badly outdated one.

Target metric: New customers who found the business through Google.

Story arc: "I didn't exist online. Now 15 people a week find my shop on Google."

### Framework 2: "From Drowning to Handled" (Time Saved)

Best for: Businesses like Maria's — overwhelmed by multiple tools and tasks.

Target metric: Time spent on marketing per week (before vs. after).

Story arc: "I used to spend 6 hours a week on marketing I hated. Now I spend 3 minutes reading my Weekly Digest."

### Framework 3: "From Hoping to Knowing" (Revenue Attribution)

Best for: Businesses like Kevin's — skeptical, pragmatic, needs to see the math.

Target metric: Revenue directly attributed to LocalGenius leads.

Story arc: "In 90 days, 23 customers called my LocalGenius tracking number. That's $8,000 in jobs. I pay $29/month."

### Case Study Rules

- Real names, real businesses, real numbers. No anonymization unless legally required.
- Photos of the actual business, not stock.
- Maximum 500 words. If the case study needs more than 500 words, the story isn't clear enough.
- Always include the "before" numbers. Without the before, the after is meaningless.
- Never say "results may vary." Instead: "Here's what happened for Rosa. Want to see what we can do for you?"

## 7. Channel-Specific Guidelines

### Facebook / Instagram Ads

**Purpose:** Awareness and consideration. Top of funnel.

**Emotional register:** Fear (awareness) → Curiosity (consideration)

**Format:** Single image or short video (<15 seconds). Image shows before/after of a real business's Google listing or website.

**Copy structure:**
- Hook (competitive fear): "The restaurant down the street has 147 more reviews than you."
- Bridge (curiosity): "Here's what happened when Rosa fixed that."
- CTA (low barrier): "See where your business stands. 60 seconds."

**Rules:**
- Never use "AI" in ad copy.
- Always reference a specific number ("147 reviews" not "more reviews").
- Always include the business type ("restaurant," "barbershop," "salon") — vertical specificity converts higher than generic.
- Test fear hooks vs. curiosity hooks — don't assume. Measure.

### Google Search Ads

**Purpose:** Capture intent. People searching for help.

**Emotional register:** Direct, helpful, specific.

**Target keywords:** "restaurant marketing help," "how to get more Google reviews," "small business marketing tool," "get found on Google Maps"

**Headline structure:** [Specific benefit] — [For business type] — [Low barrier]
> "Get 50+ Google Reviews in 90 Days — For Restaurants — Free Setup in 5 Min"

**Description structure:** [Empathy] + [Differentiator] + [CTA]
> "You opened a restaurant, not a marketing agency. LocalGenius handles your reviews, website, and social posts — you just approve. Try it free."

**Rules:**
- Never bid on competitor brand names in v1. We're too small to win that war.
- Focus on problem-aware keywords ("how to respond to bad reviews"), not product-aware keywords ("best marketing tool").
- Landing pages must match ad specificity — a "Google reviews" ad goes to a "Google reviews" landing page, not the homepage.

### In-App Copy

**Purpose:** Guide, inform, build trust. This is the in-product voice — the daily colleague.

**Emotional register:** Warm, competent, brief.

**Rules:**
- First person ("I posted your update") not third person ("Your update was posted").
- Maximum one sentence per notification. If it needs more, it's a thread message, not a notification.
- Every action card has exactly two buttons: a primary action ("Approve," "Post Now") and a secondary action ("Edit," "Skip"). Never three buttons.
- Celebrate milestones briefly: "You hit 50 reviews. Nice." Not "🎉 Congratulations!! You've reached an AMAZING milestone! 🎉"
- Error messages are calm and solution-first: "Your Instagram connection dropped. I'll reconnect it — should be back in a few minutes." Never: "Error: Instagram API authentication failed (code 401)."

### Email Campaigns

**Purpose:** Nurture (pre-signup), welcome (first week), digest (ongoing), re-engagement (lapsed).

**Emotional register varies by type:**
- Nurture: Curiosity + gentle urgency
- Welcome: Relief + delight
- Digest: Pride + partnership (mirrors the in-app Weekly Digest)
- Re-engagement: Calm update, not guilt trip

**Subject line rules:**
- Maximum 6 words.
- No emoji in subject lines.
- Personalization with business name, not owner name: "Here's what we found about [Maria's Kitchen]" — the business is the character.
- Never use "Don't miss out" or "Last chance" or "Act now." Maria gets 40 emails like that a day from vendors.

**Body rules:**
- Maximum 150 words for nurture emails.
- Maximum 250 words for digest emails.
- One CTA per email. One. If you can't decide which CTA, the email isn't focused enough.
- Always end with what happens next, not what Maria should do: "I'll check in next week with an update" — not "Click here to learn more!"

### The Weekly Digest (email version)

**Same content as in-app Digest.** Formatted for email:
- Subject line: "This week at [Business Name]: [one notable number]"
  - Example: "This week at Maria's Kitchen: 23 bookings"
- Pre-header: The first sentence of Act 1.
- Body: Three acts (What Happened, What I Did, What I Recommend) — identical to in-app.
- CTA: "Open LocalGenius" button — takes her to the app if installed, to the web version if not.
- Footer: Minimal. Unsubscribe link. No social icons. No "follow us." The email is FROM LocalGenius, not about LocalGenius.

### Review Response Drafts

**Purpose:** Provide the owner with a ready-to-post review response that sounds like them, not like a corporation.

**Emotional register:** Gracious (positive reviews), empathetic + accountable (negative reviews).

**Positive review response formula:**
> "Thanks, [Reviewer Name] — [specific acknowledgment of what they mentioned]. [Warm invitation to return]."
> Example: "Thanks, Jake — glad you loved the brisket tacos. They're Maria's favorite too. See you next time."

**Negative review response formula:**
> "[Acknowledgment without defensiveness]. [Specific response to their concern]. [Invitation to make it right]."
> Example: "Sarah, I'm sorry about the wait last Tuesday. We had an unusually busy night and your experience didn't reflect what we aim for. I'd love to make it up to you — next time you visit, ask for Maria."

**Rules:**
- Never use the business owner's name unless they've opted in. Some owners prefer anonymity in review responses.
- Never argue with the reviewer. Never.
- Negative reviews always get flagged for owner approval. The AI drafts, the owner decides.
- Keep responses under 50 words. Long responses look defensive.
- Match the reviewer's energy. If they wrote one sentence, respond with one sentence. If they wrote a paragraph, respond with two sentences (not a paragraph — brevity signals confidence).
