## How to Use This Document

These personas are not demographic profiles. They are people. They have names, days, fears, and moments when they almost gave up. Every product decision, every piece of copy, every feature prioritization should start with: "What would this feel like to Maria? To Darnell? To Kevin?"

If you're building something and you can't name which person it's for, stop building it.

## 1. Primary Persona: Maria Gonzalez

**The restaurant owner who is great at food and terrible at marketing — and knows it.**

### Who She Is

Maria is 43. She owns Maria's Kitchen, a Tex-Mex restaurant on South Lamar in Austin. She opened it seven years ago with her husband Carlos after spending a decade as a sous chef at other people's restaurants. They have 11 employees — six kitchen, four front-of-house, one part-time bookkeeper. Revenue: $680,000 last year, up from $620,000 the year before, but margins are thinning because food costs keep climbing.

Maria is not stupid. She graduated from the Culinary Institute of America. She manages a complex operation — food safety, staffing, vendor negotiations, health inspections — with competence and grit. But when it comes to marketing, she feels like she's drowning in a language she doesn't speak.

### What Her Day Looks Like

5:30am — Maria's alarm goes off. She checks her phone: 3 notifications from Yelp, 2 from Instagram, 1 from Google. She swipes them all away. She'll deal with them "later."

6:30am — Arrives at the restaurant. Receives produce delivery. Checks inventory. Preps the day's specials.

11:00am — Lunch rush begins. For the next three hours, she's expediting orders, covering for a sick line cook, and talking to regulars.

2:30pm — Brief lull. She sits down with her phone. She sees a new Google review — 2 stars. "Food was okay but we waited 40 minutes." Her stomach drops. She knows she should respond but doesn't know what to say. She writes three drafts. Deletes all of them. She'll deal with it "later."

3:30pm — Dinner prep begins.

6:00pm — Dinner rush. She's on her feet until 9:30pm.

10:00pm — Maria sits in her car in the parking lot. She's exhausted. She opens Instagram. Her competitor, a trendy taco place that opened six months ago, has 4,200 followers. Maria has 380. Their last post was today — a gorgeous photo of a margarita flight. Maria's last post was 11 weeks ago. She stares at the screen for a minute, then closes the app.

10:15pm — She opens the Yelp notification from this morning. Another 2-star review. She closes the app. She drives home.

This is Maria's relationship with digital marketing. It's not that she doesn't care. It's that caring makes it worse, because caring means seeing how far behind she is.

### What She's Tried

- **Hired her nephew to "do social media"** (2023). He posted for two weeks, then went back to college. She paid him $300 for 6 Instagram posts, 4 of which had typos.
- **Signed up for Mailchimp** (2024). Spent 90 minutes trying to create an email template. Couldn't figure out how to import her customer list. Closed the tab. Still gets Mailchimp emails she doesn't open.
- **Paid a local marketing agency** ($1,200/month for 3 months in 2024). They produced generic posts that didn't sound like her restaurant. "Indulge in our delectable offerings!" — Maria would never say that. She cancelled after 3 months. That's $3,600 she'll never get back.
- **Asked her daughter (19) for help.** Her daughter set up a Linktree and posted a few TikToks. Got some views. But her daughter has her own life, and Maria felt guilty asking.

### What She Fears

- That the taco place across the street is going to take her customers because they "do social media right"
- That a bad Google review will be the first thing new patients see when they search for restaurants nearby
- That she's being unprofessional — her website (if she even has one) looks like it was made in 2015 because it was
- That she'll spend money on another marketing thing that doesn't work
- That she's falling behind in a way she can't catch up from

### What Would Make Her Cry With Relief

Opening an app and seeing that someone — something — already responded to that 2-star review. Kindly. Professionally. In a tone that sounds like her. And then seeing that her Google Business Profile has been updated with her new weekend hours. And that a post went out today featuring her Wednesday special, with a photo she took last week.

All of it done. Without her asking. While she was cooking.

She would text Carlos: "You have to see this."

### Decision Trigger

Maria will try LocalGenius when one of two things happens:
1. **A peer recommendation.** Another restaurant owner at a Chamber of Commerce mixer shows her their Weekly Digest on their phone. "Look at this — it does everything. I haven't thought about marketing in two months." Maria trusts people who run businesses like hers, not ads.
2. **A fear trigger.** She sees a competitor's marketing that makes her feel inadequate. She Googles "help with restaurant marketing" at 10pm in her car. She finds LocalGenius. The landing page says: "Your competitor across the street has 147 more Google reviews than you. We can fix that in 5 minutes." She signs up before she talks herself out of it.

### Objections

- **"I've been burned before."** The agency took her money and produced generic crap. She needs proof immediately — not a free trial, not a sales call. Real output in her hands in five minutes.
- **"I don't have time to learn a new tool."** If onboarding takes more than one sitting, she's gone. She doesn't have a "next time." There's only now or never.
- **"I can't afford another monthly charge."** $29/month is fine if it works. She spent $1,200/month on something that didn't. But she needs to see value before her second billing cycle.
- **"What if it posts something embarrassing?"** Trust is the core issue. The approval gate in v1 (confirm before posting) is non-negotiable for Maria. She needs to feel in control until she's ready to let go.

### What "Success" Feels Like

Maria doesn't measure success in impressions or engagement rates. Success feels like:
- Not dreading the moment she opens Google reviews
- Having her daughter say "Mom, your Instagram actually looks good now"
- A new customer saying "I found you on Google" and Maria thinking "that's because my listing is actually up to date"
- Monday mornings when the Weekly Digest arrives and she reads it in 90 seconds and thinks: "Everything's handled."
- The absence of guilt. The feeling that she's not falling behind anymore.

### Maria's v1 Feature Priority

1. Review autopilot (the thing that hurts most)
2. Website generation (the thing that embarrasses most)
3. Social posting (the thing she neglects most)
4. Weekly Digest (the thing that gives her peace of mind)
5. Email/SMS campaigns (later — she doesn't have a customer list yet)

## 2. Secondary Persona: Darnell Williams

**The second-generation barbershop owner who inherited his father's reputation but not his father's marketing immunity.**

### Who He Is

Darnell is 34. He runs Williams & Sons Barbershop in East Austin, a shop his father opened in 1991. His father built the business on word of mouth and a chair at the same corner for 30 years. No website. No Instagram. No Google listing. The phone rang because everyone in the neighborhood knew where to find Earl Williams.

Darnell took over three years ago when his father retired. The neighborhood has changed. Half the old regulars moved to Round Rock. New people are moving in — tech workers, young families — and they don't know Earl Williams. They search "barbershop near me" on their phone, and Williams & Sons doesn't show up because it doesn't exist online.

Darnell is digitally comfortable — he's on Instagram personally, he watches YouTube, he knows how to use his phone. But he's a barber, not a marketer. The tools he's tried — Booksy, Square Appointments, a Wix site he started but never finished — all felt like they were built for someone else. Too many options. Too much configuration. Too much assumption that he knows what a "sales funnel" is or why he'd want one.

### What His Day Looks Like

7:00am — Opens the shop. Sweeps. Checks the schedule (paper book, not an app — his father's book, his father's handwriting still in some margins).

8:00am — First client. Darnell talks to every person in his chair. He knows their kids' names, their jobs, their teams. The barbershop is a community center as much as a business.

12:00pm — Between clients, he scrolls Instagram. He sees other barbershops with 15K followers posting transformation reels with professional lighting. He thinks about posting but doesn't know where to start and doesn't want to look like he's trying too hard.

5:00pm — Last client. He closes up and thinks about the three new barbershops that opened in a two-mile radius in the last year. All of them have Google listings with professional photos. His shop doesn't have a Google listing at all.

### How He Discovers LocalGenius

Darnell is at a barbering event — a continuing education day sponsored by a product vendor. Another shop owner, slightly older, shows him the LocalGenius app on his phone. "Look, it set up my Google listing and now I get 15 calls a week from new people. I didn't do anything." Darnell downloads it that night.

### Why Existing Tools Feel Wrong

Every tool Darnell has tried assumed he was starting a startup:
- Booksy wanted him to set up "services" with "durations" and "pricing tiers." He cuts hair. It takes how long it takes. It costs $30.
- Wix showed him 47 templates and asked him to choose fonts. He closed the tab.
- Square sent him emails about "customer relationship management." He's standing three feet from his customers six days a week. He IS the customer relationship management.

LocalGenius works for Darnell because it doesn't ask him to be a marketer. It says: "Tell me about your shop. I'll handle the rest." It speaks in results ("23 people found your shop this week"), not in jargon ("your SEO profile has improved 34%").

### What "Success" Feels Like for Darnell

- New customers walking in saying "I found you on Google Maps"
- A full Saturday book with a mix of old regulars and new faces
- His father seeing the shop's Google listing and saying "Huh. Nice."
- Not thinking about marketing at all, the same way his father never had to

### Darnell's v1 Feature Priority

1. Google Business Profile setup and optimization (he literally doesn't exist online)
2. Website generation (so "Williams & Sons barbershop Austin" actually finds something)
3. Review management (he has zero reviews — needs to ask his regulars)
4. Social posting (secondary — he cares more about being findable than being followed)

## 3. Churn-Risk Persona: Kevin Tran

**The solo plumber who will cancel in 90 days unless you show him exactly where his money went.**

### Who He Is

Kevin is 38. He's a licensed master plumber who left a commercial plumbing company two years ago to go solo. He operates out of his truck. No storefront. No employees. Revenue: $140,000 last year, up from $95,000 his first year, but every dollar is a fight.

Kevin is pragmatic to the bone. He doesn't do feelings, he does math. He spent $300/month on Google Ads for six months and couldn't tell if it worked because he couldn't track which phone calls came from ads and which came from his yard signs. He cancelled the ads.

He's on NextDoor. He's on Thumbtack. He has a Google Business Profile that he set up himself (basic, but it exists). His reviews are strong — 4.7 stars, 38 reviews — because he's genuinely excellent at his job and asks every customer to leave a review.

### Why Kevin Will Churn

Kevin doesn't need marketing *help*. He needs marketing *proof*. If LocalGenius costs $29/month and he can't see exactly which jobs came from LocalGenius, he'll cancel before the 90-day mark. Guaranteed.

The risk factors:
- **He already has a basic digital presence.** He doesn't get the "proof moment" of seeing his business online for the first time.
- **He tracks revenue per lead source.** If he can't attribute calls to LocalGenius, it's overhead.
- **He has no employees to impress.** There's no one to text "look at this." His relationship with the product is purely transactional.
- **His margins are thin.** $29/month is $348/year. He needs at least 2 additional jobs from LocalGenius to justify the cost. One average plumbing job = $350. The math is tight.

### What Keeps Kevin

1. **Lead attribution.** A LocalGenius tracking number on his Google listing and website. "This month, 7 people called your LocalGenius number. 3 booked jobs. Estimated revenue: $1,050." If Kevin sees that number, he stays forever.

2. **Review velocity.** Kevin knows reviews = revenue. If LocalGenius can accelerate his review collection (automated "how was your experience?" texts after each job), he sees direct value. "You got 6 new reviews this month. That's 3 more than your monthly average before LocalGenius."

3. **Competitive context.** "There are 14 plumbers in your service area on Google. You're ranked #4. Here's what the top 3 do differently." Kevin is competitive. Show him the scoreboard.

4. **Low maintenance.** Kevin will never open the app more than once a week. The Weekly Digest IS his entire product experience. If it's good — meaning it shows him his numbers — he stays. If it's not, he cancels.

### What "Success" Feels Like for Kevin

- His phone rings and the caller says "I found you on Google." He knows it's a LocalGenius lead because the tracking number is different from his personal line.
- His Weekly Digest shows a number that matters: "You gained 4 new reviews and 3 new jobs this month. That's $1,050 in revenue attributed to LocalGenius." The $29 feels like the best money he spends.
- He doesn't think about LocalGenius between digests. It runs in the background like his truck insurance — he pays, it protects, he doesn't manage it.

### Kevin's v1 Feature Priority

1. Lead attribution (tracking number — this is the retention feature for Kevin)
2. Review acceleration (automated post-job review requests)
3. Google Business Profile optimization (he's already there, make him rank higher)
4. Weekly Digest with hard numbers (revenue attributed, reviews gained, rank position)
5. Everything else is noise to him

## 4. Future Persona: Linda Chen

**The franchise operator who will pay $79/location — but not until we're ready for her.**

### Who She Is

Linda is 52. She operates seven Supercuts franchise locations across the Austin metro. Revenue: $3.2M annually across all locations. She has a regional manager and 35 employees total.

Linda currently pays a marketing agency $4,500/month to manage social media and reputation across all seven locations. The agency is... fine. They post generic content. They respond to reviews with templates. Every location's Instagram looks the same despite serving different neighborhoods.

Linda is not our v1 customer. But she's the reason the data model is multi-tenant from day one (locked Decision #4).

### Why She Matters (Future)

- **Economics:** 7 locations × $79/month = $553/month per Linda. One Linda = 19 Marias.
- **Consistency problem:** She needs each location to have a local voice (the Supercuts in East Austin serves a different community than the one in Lakeway) while maintaining brand consistency. This is a genuinely hard problem that agencies solve badly.
- **Switching cost:** Once Linda migrates 7 locations to LocalGenius, she's locked in. Her churn rate will be a fraction of Kevin's.

### What She'd Need (Not Building Yet)

- Multi-location dashboard (see all 7 at a glance)
- Location-level customization with brand guardrails
- Regional manager access (delegated permissions)
- Consolidated billing
- Cross-location performance comparison

We document Linda now so the team knows where the product goes. We don't build for her until solo-operator retention is proven.

## 5. Anti-Personas — Who We Are NOT Building For

### Rachel — The Marketing Agency Owner

Rachel runs a 12-person digital marketing agency. She manages 40 client accounts. She wants white-label tools, API access, bulk scheduling, and detailed analytics. She evaluates tools based on feature comparison spreadsheets.

**Why she's an anti-persona:** Everything Rachel needs would make the product worse for Maria. Agency features (multi-client dashboards, white-label options, API access) are complexity that our primary users would never touch. Building for Rachel means building a different product.

### Jason — The Tech-Savvy Founder

Jason is 29. He runs a direct-to-consumer hot sauce brand. He has 12K Instagram followers he grew himself. He uses Buffer, Canva, Mailchimp, and Google Analytics. He knows what CTR means. He wants granular control over every post, every A/B test, every audience segment.

**Why he's an anti-persona:** Jason doesn't need someone in his corner — he IS his own someone. LocalGenius would feel limiting to him. He'd want to see the prompt that generated his copy. He'd want to override the AI's scheduling decisions. He'd want a dashboard. Every feature that satisfies Jason's desire for control erodes the simplicity that Maria needs.

### Patricia — The Enterprise Brand Manager

Patricia manages digital presence for a hotel chain with 200 locations. She needs enterprise SSO, SOC 2 compliance, custom reporting, and a dedicated account manager. Her procurement process takes 6 months.

**Why she's an anti-persona:** Patricia operates in a different universe. Her buying process, her scale, her compliance requirements, her team structure — none of it maps to LocalGenius. Even Linda (7 locations) is a stretch for v1. Patricia is a distraction dressed up as an opportunity.

| **Local SEO** | High — she needs to show up when someone searches "Mexican restaurant near me." | Critical — he literally doesn't appear in Google Maps. | High — "plumber near me" searches are his lifeline. | Future — 7 locations competing for 7 different local markets. |

| **Lead Attribution (Tracking Number)** | Low — she doesn't think in terms of lead sources. | Low — walk-ins don't come from tracked channels. | Critical — THE retention feature for Kevin. No tracking = no Kevin. | Future — per-location tracking and consolidated reporting. |
| **Approval Gate** | Essential — she needs to trust before she lets go. Approval gate = safety net. | Important — he wants to see what goes out but will trust quickly. | Low — he barely engages. Auto-pilot is fine for him from day one. | Future — regional manager approves, brand guardrails enforce. |

### What This Map Tells Us

1. **Maria and Darnell share the same core product.** Their priorities differ slightly (Maria leads with reviews, Darnell leads with SEO/presence), but they need the same features in the same wrapper.

2. **Kevin needs a different value proposition.** His retention hinges on one feature: lead attribution. If we don't build the tracking number by month 2, every Kevin churns.

3. **Linda validates the architecture, not the v1 feature set.** Her existence justifies the multi-tenant data model. Her features are a future product.

4. **The Weekly Digest is universal.** Every persona relies on it. It's the one feature that must be excellent for all three v1 personas — but it needs to adapt what it shows to each person's priority.
